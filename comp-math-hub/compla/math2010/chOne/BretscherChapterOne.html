Linear Equations

1.1 Systems of Linear Equations

  • Solving systems of linear equation is something you do in elementary algebra and linear algebra. Often times, you do algebraic manipulations such as subtracting terms from an equation.

$\begin{vmatrix} x + 2y + 3z = 8 \\ x + 3y + 3z = 10 \\ x + 2y + 4z = 9\end{vmatrix}$

  • subtract equation I from equations II and III
    • $x - x + 3y - 2y + 3z - 3z = 10 - 8 \rightarrow y = 2 $
    • $x - x + 2y - 2y + 4z - 3z = 9 - 8 \rightarrow z = 1 $

$ \begin{vmatrix} x + 2y + 3z = 8 \\ 0 + y + 0 = 2 \\ 0 + 0 + z = 1\end{vmatrix} $

  • subtract 2 II from I and subtract 3 III from I
    • $x + 2y - 2y + 3z = 8 -4 \rightarrow x + 3z = 4 $
    • $x + 3z -3z = 4 -3 \rightarrow x = 1$

$ \begin{vmatrix} x + 0 + 0 = 1 \\ 0 + y + 0 = 2 \\ 0 + 0 + z = 1\end{vmatrix} $

  • Hence the final solution is $\begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$

Geometric Interpretation

  • The geometric interpretation of a system of equations such as the one above is that we have 3 planes intersecting at one point (1,2,1).

Systems with No Solutions

  • When the system is inconsistent there are no solutions. In other words, all the planes of the system of equations do not meet at a single point, line, or plane.

  • For example, $\begin{vmatrix} x + 2y + 3z = 1 \\ x + 3y + 4z = 3 \\ x +4y + 5z = 4 \end{vmatrix}$

  • Subtract equation I from equation II and subtract equation I from equation II.

    • $x - x + 3y - 2y + 4z -3z = 3 - 1 \longrightarrow y + z = 2$
    • $x - x + 4y - 2y + 5z -3z = 4 - 1 \longrightarrow y + z = \frac{3}{2}$
    • $\begin{vmatrix} x + 2y + 3z = 1 \\ 0 + y + z = 2 \\ 0 + y + z = \frac{3}{2} \end{vmatrix}$
      • Since y + z equates to different values, we can see that the system does not have any solution. However, if you want a more concrete piece of evidence subtract II from III
      • $\begin{vmatrix} x + 2y + 3z = 1 \\ 0 + y + z = 2 \\ 0 + 0 + 0 = -\frac{1}{2} \end{vmatrix}$
      • O cannot be equal -0.5; It is a contradiction.

Systems with Infinitely Many Solutions

  • When the system is consistent and there is a free variable, there are infinitely many solutions.

  • For example, $\begin{vmatrix} x + 2y + 3z = 1 \\ 3x + 2y + z = 1 \\ 7x + 2y -3z = 1 \end{vmatrix}$

    • Subtract 3I from II and subtract 7I from III
      • $II - 3I \rightarrow 3x -3x + 2y -6y + z - 9z = 1 -3 \rightarrow 0 - 4y -8y = -2 \rightarrow y + 2z = \frac{1}{2}$
      • $III - 7I \rightarrow 7x -7x + 2y -14y - 3z - 21z = 1 -7 \rightarrow 0 - 12y -24z = -6 \rightarrow y + 2z = \frac{1}{2}$

  • Since II and III are equivalent subtract II from III

    • Hence we get a free variable which in this case is z because x and y are pivot values or the first non-zero value of a row.
    • $\begin{vmatrix} x + 2y + 3z = 1 \\ 0 + y + 2z = \frac{1}{2} \\ 0 + 0 + 0 = 0 \end{vmatrix}$
    • $x = 1 -2y - 3z \\ y = \frac{1}{2} - 2z \\ z = t, \; f\!or \; t \in \mathbb{R} \\ \rightarrow x = 1 -2(\frac{1}{2} -2t) -3t = 1 - 1 + 4t -3t = t \\ y = \frac{1}{2} - 2t \\ z = t \\ \rightarrow \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} t \\ \frac{1}{2} -t \\ t \end{bmatrix}$

  • As you can see above, the variable z is set to some arbitrary real scalar value or real number t since it is the free variable.

1.2 Matrices, Vectors, and Gauss-Jordan Elimination

  • Matrices have $m$ rows and $n$ columns
  • for instance let A be 3x2 matrix with real values as its entries.

$A = \begin{bmatrix} a_{11} & a_{12} \\ a_{21} & a_{22} \\ a_{31} & a_{32} \end{bmatrix}$ where $a_{ij} \in \mathbb{R}$

  • A is called a square matrix if its number of rows and columns are the same.
    • let A be a $n \times n$ matrix where $n \in \mathbb{Z}^{non-negative} \\ A = \begin{bmatrix} a_{11} & a_{12} & \dots & a_{1 \; n-1} & a_{1n} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots \\ a_{n-1 \; 1} & a_{n-1 \; 2} & \dots & a_{n-1 \; n-1} & a_{n-1 \; n} \\ a_{n1} & a_{n2} & \dots & a_{n \; n-1} & a_{nn} \end{bmatrix}$
$$$$
  • A square matrix is called diagonal if all its entries above and below the main diagonal are zero. In other words, $a_{ij} = 0$ whenever $i \neq j$
    • Example let a be a 2x2 diagonal matrix with real entries $A = \begin{bmatrix} a_{11} & 0 \\ 0 & a_{22} \end{bmatrix}$, where $a_{ij} = 0$ if $i \neq j$
$$$$
  • A square matrix A is called upper triangular if all its entries below the main diagonal are zero.
    • Example let a be a 3x3 upper triangular matrix with real entries $A = \begin{bmatrix} a_{11} & a_{12} & a_{13} \\ 0 & a_{22} & a_{23} \\ 0 & 0 & a_{33} \end{bmatrix}$, where $a_{ij} = 0$ whenever i exceeds j (i > j) or the index number of the row exceeds the index number of the column.
$$$$

  • A square matrix is called lower triangular is all its entries above the main diagonal are zero.
    • Example let a be a 3x3 lower triangular matrix with real entries $A = \begin{bmatrix} a_{11} & 0 & 0 \\ a_{12} & a_{22} & 0 \\ a_{31} & a_{32} & a_{33} \end{bmatrix}$, where $a_{ij} = 0$ whenever j exceeds 1 or the index number of the column exceeds the index number of the row.
  • A zero matrix is a matrix for which all entries are 0
    • Ex $A = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \\ \\ B = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0\end{bmatrix} $

Vectors and Vector Spaces

  • A column vector is a matrix with only one column.
    • The set of all column vectors with n components is denoted by $\mathbb{R}^n$
    • $\mathbb{R}^n$ is a vector space
  • A row vector is a matrix with only one row.
    • The set of all row vectors with n components is denoted by $\mathbb{R}^n$
    • $\mathbb{R}^n$ is a vector space

1.3 On Solutions of Linear Systems

Number of solutions of a linear system

- A system of equation is said to be consistent if there is at least one solution; it is inconsistent if there are no solutions. ¶

  • Theorem
    • A linear system is inconsistent if and only if a contradiction such as $0 = c$, where $c \neq 0$ arises. Usually, when the RREF for a augmented matrix contains a row $\begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 & \dots & 0 \vdots 1 \end{bmatrix}$
    • If a linear system is consistent, then it has either
      • Infinitely many solutions (if there is at least one free variable)
      • exactly one solution (if all columns have a pivot entry)
  • Definition: The rank of a matrix is the number of leading ones in rref($A$), denoted rank($A$)

Ex

$I = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$

- rank(I) = 3 because there is 1 leading ones at each row.

$A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$

- rank(A) = 2 because there are 2 leading ones in two rows.

Number of equations vs. number of unknowns


  1. If a linear system has exactly one solution, then there must be at least as many equations as there are variables ($m \le n$ with the notation from example 3).

    • $p \rightarrow q$, where p = a linear system has one solution and q = there must be at least as many equations as there are variables ($m \; (rows) \; \le \; n \; (columns))$;

  1. (contrapositive of 1 )A linear system with fewer equations than unknowns ($n < m$) has either no solutions or infinitely many solutions.

    • ($\neg q \rightarrow \neg p$), where where p = a linear system has one solution and q = there must be at least as many equations as there are variables ($m \; (rows) \; \le \; n \; (columns)\;)$;

Systems of n equations in n variables

  • A linear system of n equations in nvariables has a unique solution if and only if the rank of its coefficient matrix $A$ is n
$$rref(A) = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 & \dots & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 & \dots & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & \dots & 0 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & \dots & 1\end{bmatrix}$$

Matrix Algebra



Algebraic rules for $A\vec{x}$

  • if A is an $n \times m$ matrix, $\vec{x}$ and $\vec{y}$ are vectors in $\mathbb{R}^m$, and k is a scalar, then
    • $A(\vec{x} + \vec{y}) = A\vec{x} + A\vec{y}$
    • $A(k\vec{x}) = k(A\vec{x})$
  • Theorem: Matrix for of a linear system
    • We can write the linear system with augmented matrix $\left [ \begin{array}{ c : c} A & \vec{b} \end{array} \right ]$ in matrix form as $A\vec{x} = \vec{b}$


  • Ex $$\left[ \begin{array}{ccc:c} x & 2 & 3 & 1 \\ 3 & 2 & 1 & 1 \\ 7 & 2 & -3 & 1 \end{array}\right] $$ can be written as $$ \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 3 & 2 & 1 \\ 7 & 2 & -3 \end{bmatrix}\vec{x} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$$, where

    $A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 3 & 2 & 1 \\ 7 & 2 & -3 \end{bmatrix}$ and $\vec{b} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} $
